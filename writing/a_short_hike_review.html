A Short Hike is an indie adventure and exploration game. I played it recently and I want to look at how the game is designed and why it's fun. We'll discuss how the game works, its mechanics, and even some of the story elements -- so, there's gonna be spoilers here. If you're looking for a relaxed time, go and play this game now (it's actually short)!

You play as Claire, a bird with the ability to climb and glide. Your goal is to reach the top of Hawk Peak so you can get cell phone reception and make an important call. The game has lots of other small objectives on the side ranging from collection to exploration and quests. As you do these things you explore a beautiful island with different sections. Just look at it. Isn't it pretty?

Core Loop: Explore, Collect, Climb

The core challenge in a short hike is the climbing. You can climb using collected gold feathers. As you get more feathers you get more stamina and can climb longer. Naturally, the more feathers you get the more certain areas and cliffs become accessible to you. If I had to describe the loop in it's simplest form it would be Explore -> Colled -> Climb. Even though the game is about wandering around an island it's remakably focused on that core loop. I separate out climbing from exploring because climbing and tiers of climbing is what gates sections of explorable content. Collecting is often the end goal of exploration, it's the consequence of it. I wanted to separate them because climbing is also the end product of exploration. You have to explore to progress through the game and find new areas you can climb to.

The climbing can involve directly scaling toward an area or cliff, or hopping An added challenge to the climbing is the camera angle which can change the way your directional controls affect the character.

Climbing like this is like wrestling with a puzzle. When you solve that mini puzzle and reach a new area it's satisfying and fun.

The game teaches you about exploration right at the start. You get two choices: talk to a character (ranger may) or run down the beach where you discover a seashell. This subtle choice indicates immediately to the player that there may be rewards tied to exploration. It also lefts you know that there may be a tradeoff between exploring and progressing the story. I thought the initial discovery of the seashell was really encouraging so I started swimming out into the ocean instead of following the main story. I discovered a set of islands with many little rewards scattered on them. While you're exploring the camera does a lot of the work by zooming out or in, changing angles to present exactly the amount of information the developer wanted at every point. This isn't something you really notice when you're immersed but since I'm playing games to understand the design and tricks I felt like it was a subtle way to guide me at various points without forcing me to do anything ever.

There's a nice synergy between the core and exploration. The more time you take to explore, the more feather's you'll have (you can find them in the world or buy them with collected money) and the easier it becomes to navigate different areas. This lets you to explore more, or blow through the climbing challenge at the core of the gameplay loop.

Why is this fun?

Progression is Tasteful and Steady

The core loop doles out progression well. As you explore you gain power, as you gain power you can explore. In the game there's often a sense of exponential progression. As you acquire access to better traversability around the island you feel like you've progressed. You can go higher, or use new bouncy flowers, or reach areas previopusly off limits. You can also jump down and spot things you might have missed before. At the same time as you enter the cold biome at higher elevations your feathers "freeze" temporarily when you use them. This provides the player with a sense that they have grown in power and progressed while still providing a new challenge as they progress. I remember games where this type of progression was lacking and everything just got too easy as the game progressed, I remember older total way titles where this was the case and the game would turn into a slog. The tweaking and twisting of the rules as I progressed was well done here and surprised me slightly. The thing that's cool about it is it isn't just a nerf in the cold zone. It's a different mechanic. You can still use the feathers -- but they work differently now and will only recharge if you stop in a spot where Claire can get warm. It's tasteful, clever and more power systems should try and implement these kinds of progression tweaks. What is the immediate goal? -- climb to new area to explore What tasks need to be achieved? -- find chests, items How long will this take? -- maybe 3-5 min What kind of permanent progress has been achieved? -- new cosmetic design or permanent increase in power (from https://www.gamasutra.com/blogs/JoshBycer/20200306/359140/The_Importance_of_a_Well_Defined_Core_Gameplay_Loop.php) Why is this fun?

The Story is About Distracted Respite

The story is a sequence of cute little vignettes that give you the sense of being distracted. As the player you obviously play the game to be immersed or distracted from reality for a little while (we call this fun). But also playing a character who's worried about a phone call, but has the opportunity to be distracted by these little vignettes is a micro break in the 4th Wall. The painter claiming we can't tell what she's painting is also one of these breaks. Of course we can't tell what she's painting it's a tiny blob of pixels on the screen with no discernable structure. In this way the story reminds you that you're playing a game, being distracted, it reminds you how Claire must feel anticipating that phone call. Ghibli/French countryside music track. Why is this fun?

Tying it Together

The core loop is well executed, and fun to play. It's just disguised as indie adventure.
